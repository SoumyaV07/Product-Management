01- Problem Statement
Who is the user?
The Gen Z café-switcher
Urban 20–28 year olds who used to be Starbucks loyalists but are now spending ₹150–250 at independent cafés instead. They find Starbucks too corporate, too predictable, and no longer worth the premium for a commodity experience.
What's the pain point?
Starbucks feels like a utility, not a treat.
The mobile-order-and-pickup model turned Starbucks stores into fulfillment centers. 60%+ of US orders are mobile pickup. The "third place" experience- the reason people paid $7 for coffee- has disappeared. There's nothing to come in for anymore.
User research signal
Reddit, Twitter/X, and Google reviews analysis across India + US: top complaints are "feels like a fast food pickup," "overpriced for what it is now," and "local cafes just feel more special." The product problem: Starbucks lost its experience identity while keeping premium pricing.
02- The Idea
Product concept
The "Black & Green" Collab Menu
A limited-run menu where Starbucks crafts drinks using Diet Coke as a base- cold brew Diet Coke floats, espresso Diet Coke tonics, sparkling coffee lemonades. Sold exclusively with a personalised Diet Coke × Starbucks tumbler- limited edition, different designs every season, engraved with your name. The tumbler is the product. The drink is the excuse to buy it. This gives people a reason to physically walk in, wait, photograph, and post- which is the experience Starbucks lost.

Risk

Diet Coke's brand team may not align on messaging- they're a separate company with their own brand guidelines. Partnership negotiation could delay launch by 3–4 months.

What I'd iterate on

If tumbler attach rate is below 25%, I'd A/B test a lower-priced "mini tumbler" at ₹499 vs the full ₹799. The goal is to get it in hands- then upsell the next season's drop.

📖 What This Taught Me About PM Thinking
The instinct here was to solve the "café competition" problem with a price cut. But that's a race to the bottom. The real PM insight is that Starbucks doesn't have a price problem- it has an experience problem. The collab menu doesn't reduce the price. It increases the value of showing up. That's the difference between marketing thinking and product thinking.

01- Problem Statement
Who is the user?
The free-tier loyalist in India
18–30 year old Indian Spotify user who has used the app for 2+ years but has never paid. They tolerate ads. They see Premium features like "no ads" and "offline listening" as nice-to-have, not must-have. YouTube Music is literally free and pre-installed- why would they pay?
The core problem
Spotify's Premium benefits don't create FOMO.
Sound quality, offline mode, ad-free- these are functional upgrades. But Indian Gen Z doesn't upgrade for function. They upgrade for identity and exclusivity. Spotify needs a Premium benefit that makes free users feel like they're missing out on something real- not just missing out on convenience.
02- The Idea
Product concept
"Artist Picks"- Celebrity Curated Playlists with Content Protection
A Premium-only playlist series where celebrities curate personal playlists- Ranbir Kapoor's midnight playlist, Virat Kohli's training mix, Dua Lipa's guilty pleasures. The UX is designed to prevent leaking: no screenshots of the tracklist, songs play in shuffle-only order, only the celebrity's face is shown on the queue screen. Free users can see the playlist exists and see who made it- but can't see or play any song. It's the locked room that makes Premium feel like a VIP pass, not just an ad-blocker.

The lesson here is that Premium benefits need to create loss aversion, not just added convenience. "Ad-free" tells you what you gain. A locked celebrity playlist tells you what you're missing. Loss aversion is 2–3x more motivating than gain framing in conversion psychology. This feature is designed around that principle.

01- Problem Statement
Who is the user?
The culturally-rooted urban sneakerhead
25–38 year olds in cities like Mumbai, Jaipur, New York, and Istanbul who have strong pride in where they're from. They buy streetwear as identity. Adidas Samba feels like a shoe you could buy in a café in Milan. They want something that feels like their street.
The problem
Nike makes global shoes. People want local ones.
Nike's current collaborations are with athletes and celebrities- both globally generic. There is no Nike shoe that makes someone from Chandigarh feel seen. Adidas hasn't cracked this either. The lane is completely empty, and Nike has the manufacturing scale and design talent to own it.
02- The Idea
Product concept
Cities & Routes- A Shoe With a Story Built In
A limited product line where each shoe is designed around a specific city or route. "New York Midnight"- dark, metallic, subway-grate texture on the sole, a skyline silhouette stitched into the heel. "Kashmir Route"- hand-embroidery-inspired prints in papier-mâché colours, Chinar leaf motif on the tongue. "Mumbai Local"- chaos and colour, train-ticket aesthetic, rain-resistant sole. Each shoe ships with a small printed "route card" that tells the story of the city- and a QR code to an AR experience where you walk the actual route on your phone. Limited quantities per city. People from that city get early access.

08- Risks & Iterations
Risk

Cultural inauthenticity is the biggest danger. If the Kashmir shoe looks like a tourist souvenir rather than a genuine cultural product, the backlash will be severe- and fast on social media.

What I'd iterate on

That's counterintuitive for a brand used to thinking at global scale- but it's the right product move.

01- Problem Statement
Who is the user?
The Oreo ritual lover + the gift-giver
Two users: (1) the 16–28 year old who has a strong personal Oreo ritual- always twists, licks the cream first, has a preferred cream thickness. Oreo's own brand identity is built on personalisation rituals- but the product doesn't let you personalise the most important thing: how much cream. (2) The 22–35 year old looking for a fun, memorable gift that isn't boring.
The core insight
Oreo's brand IS the ritual. Why not sell the ritual itself?
Oreo has run years of "how do you eat your Oreo?" campaigns. The brand already knows people care deeply about the cream. But the product gives you no control over the cream. You get what you get. The Build Your Own Kit closes the gap between the brand's promise (your Oreo, your way) and the product's reality (one standard cream amount).
02- The Idea
Product concept
The Oreo Kit- Plain Biscuits, Your Cream, Your Rules
A boxed kit containing: plain Oreo wafers (no cream) + a small glass jar of cream in your chosen flavour (classic, double, chocolate, strawberry, mint) + a mini spatula for application. You choose how thick. You choose the flavour. The jar doubles as a collectible after the cream runs out- small enough to keep on a desk or vanity. Sold online and as an in-store gifting SKU at ₹349. The spatula has "BUILD YOUR OWN" embossed on it. The first FMCG biscuit brand to let you design your own bite.

01- Problem Statement
Who is the user?
The group trip organiser
The 24–35 year old who always ends up "managing" the group trip- collating preferences over WhatsApp, fighting over who wants flights vs train, manually checking MMT for 6 different people's dates, sharing screenshots back and forth. They do all the work and still get blamed when something's wrong.
The pain point
Group travel planning is a coordination nightmare.
47% of Indian trips are group trips. But MMT has no collaborative feature. Everyone has to share screenshots, argue in chats, and trust one person to book. The result: decision fatigue, conflict, and booking abandonment. Groups often take 3–5 WhatsApp conversations and multiple app sessions to confirm a trip- or give up entirely.
User research signal
Twitter/X search "makemytrip group trip", Reddit r/india travel threads, and Google Play reviews: top complaints include "no way to plan together," "had to share screenshots with 6 people," and "ended up booking separately because it was too hard to agree." This is a known, unresolved pain point with zero current solution in the Indian OTA market.
02- The Idea
Product concept
TripSquad- Group Itinerary Planning Where AI Resolves the Fight
A collaborative trip planning mode inside the MMT app. One person creates a "Squad" and invites friends via link. Each member independently inputs: dates they're free, budget range, transport preference (flight/train/road), top 3 must-visit places. Once all members have responded, MMT's AI generates 3 itinerary options ranked by how well they satisfy the group's collective preferences- with a "compatibility score" per option. Group votes with one tap. No arguments needed. The AI shows each person why their preference was or wasn't included, with a clear trade-off explanation. The organiser books in one click. The entire group can see the live itinerary, split costs, and get individual reminders.

08- Risks & Iterations
Risk

AI itinerary quality depends on data quality. If member preferences are vague ("anywhere nice, budget okay"), the AI options will be generic and the feature will feel useless. Needs smart form design with smart defaults.

What I'd iterate on

After beta, if completion rate is below 45%, I'd add "quick mode"- just share dates and budget, AI handles the rest. Reduce cognitive load for the group members who won't fill a full form.

📖 PM Insight
The surface-level pitch is "collaborative travel planning." But the real PM insight is that this is a group conversion feature, not a UX feature. Each Squad that completes a booking represents 3–8 people booking simultaneously- that's 4× the average transaction value in one click. MMT's AI infrastructure (Myra) already exists. TripSquad is about applying it to a problem that unlocks a disproportionately large revenue opportunity. That's the kind of thinking that separates a feature spec from a product strategy.
